They are singing, their voices loud above the fires of their torches as they set their world ablaze; and that too is part of the tapestry.

A dish with three figs is placed over the table. Over the pink shift of the torn bones that rest against the dark green plates, the fruit seems out of place. The dark red linen drapes across the dark wood, itself worn and bitten at the edges by age and the insects that had found its meal in it, in the food, in the air around them. The varnish speaks of illness, as do the bones. The light shifts and the buzzing stops, their wings vanish and their small bodies disappear into the shadows. Finely aged with a single move of the day. Beautiful and rich all the same.

Another dish is placed on the table, silentely and the song is now louder; or perhaps it is just the scrapping of the little fiends carving out the wood from within. The gentle rasping of heavy paintings against darkened walls. The ripples of paint heavy with intent, but silent all the same when no eyes are on them.

He smiles and behind his closed, tight lipped lips linger the stories within the frames, the songs in the fires, the torn and worn leather book covers, the heaviness of their steps against worn stones. His index touches the tip of his thumb, the rustling of silver rings against skin, worn down gold plate shifting the skin beneath to green. Dark eyes seem to glare at him, empty and so far away. Their lips hungry, starving even then.

The Gentleman's steps are silent as he shifts his weight from one foot to the next. His wrist turns gently and fingers fall to the side of his body. The dish is picked from the table, the steps of those that served traced back accross the corridors. Another step, the figs are taken up once again - their green and plump shape sinking onto itself as it moves further and further from the table.

Dark eyes follow the figure that passes. As he stands over the window, the varnish shines brightly over the table. As brightly as the sun outside, peering through heavy windows do. There are fewer rings around his fingers, some of them of pure, strong gold, others of oxidised silver. At the base of his indicator his finger thins; a sickly methalic green around it.

Left hand holds one of the figs, rotten and deflated between long and veiny fingers. Held in space, hanging between the plate and his mouth. He places it down. Dark eyes close as the sun brushes against his face.

Zofia had been given many names along the years: from the Baroness of Czajka, the Vampire of Czajka, the Baroness and all different combinations.

The woman, however, always chose to introduce herself as Zofia.

Zofia was born in Paris after her parents were exiled to France after the failure of the November Uprising. Her first language was polish, and the one that speaks to her heart, even after learning french, english and portuguese. It would be nearly thirty years after her birth for Zofia to visit KrÃ¡kow, remaining there for a few years. It was there that she met her husband - in the university as he studied to become a doctor. It was with a heavy heart that they left KrÃ¡kow and occupied Poland all together in the later part of the 19th century. Choosing instead to exile themselves in southern europe (Portugal) for years. An extreme lucky guess and choice which kept them safe through the two world wars and had allowed her space to attempt to help fleeing refuges from both wars. Or attempt to give as much relief as one was able to.

Joining the Brotherhood came much later in life, after becoming a widow and being alone. It came with knowing that her husband had died without seeing their country become free of its shackles despite of the pain. It came after multiple accusations of cowardice from other mutants that say that she has squandared her extremely long lifespan (a side effect from her mutation that Zofia herself did not know she would have) and did nothing. She hid because it allowed her to live a life that she thought she would have, a normal life. She lived a life worth living with a man that loved her dearly and she loved in return.

She faces them with a clear conscience, knowing fully well that no hero, no martyr has been able to live happily and that she did all that she could at every turn. Even now.
